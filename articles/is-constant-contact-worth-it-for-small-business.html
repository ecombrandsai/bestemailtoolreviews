How to Make Constant Contact Pay for Itself in the First Month

Signing up is the easy part. Here is how to make sure your first month generates more than $12 in revenue.

Step 1: Import every contact you already have

Start with what you have. Export contacts from your point-of-sale system, your Gmail, your old Mailchimp account, your phone contacts app, your Facebook page's followers list. Constant Contact imports CSV files, Excel spreadsheets, and syncs directly with Gmail and Outlook contacts. Most small businesses already have 100 to 300 potential subscribers sitting in tools they use every day.

Step 2: Send a "we're back in your inbox" email within 48 hours

Do not wait until you have the perfect newsletter planned. Send a short email to your existing contacts within two days of importing them. Tell them who you are, what you sell, and include one specific offer — a 10% discount, a free consultation, first access to new inventory, or a link to book an appointment. This single email typically produces the highest open rate you will ever see because the list is fresh.

Step 3: Set up a welcome email for new subscribers

Every time someone signs up through your website form or in-store sign-up sheet, they should automatically receive a welcome email. This is a Standard plan feature, but it runs on autopilot once you set it up. A local bookshop using this tactic reported that 18% of customers who received the welcome email made a purchase within 30 days, compared to 4% who did not.

Step 4: Send at least two emails per month

Once per month is not enough. The research is consistent: two to four emails per month produce the best results for small businesses without causing unsubscribes. Plan one content-focused email (a how-to, a behind-the-scenes story, a customer spotlight) and one promotion-focused email (a sale, a new product, a seasonal offer). This schedule takes about two hours per month once you are comfortable with the tool.

Step 5: Check your reports and improve one thing

After each campaign, open your Constant Contact dashboard and look at three numbers: open rate, click rate, and unsubscribes. Industry average open rates hover around 20%. If yours is below 15%, try a different subject line next time. If your click rate is below 2%, try one clearer call-to-action button instead of three competing links. Improving one metric per campaign compounds over time.

Who Should Consider a Different Tool

Constant Contact is the right tool for most small businesses, but not every single one. Here are three situations where you might want a different option.

You need to send more than 500,000 emails per month

Constant Contact's pricing scales with contact list size. If you have an enormous list — say, 50,000+ contacts — the monthly cost climbs significantly. At that volume, a platform like Brevo, which charges per email sent rather than per contact stored, can be more cost-effective. Most small businesses will never reach this point, but it is worth knowing.

You need complex, multi-branch automation

Constant Contact's automation is solid for welcome series, birthday emails, and anniversary campaigns. But if you need sophisticated conditional logic — "if the customer clicked link A but not link B, and spent more than $50 last quarter, send them email C in 3 days but only if it is a weekday" — you would be better served by a tool like ActiveCampaign or Klaviyo. Those tools cost more and require more setup time.

Your entire budget is zero dollars

If you genuinely cannot spend $12 per month right now, MailerLite and Brevo both offer free plans with basic functionality. They are not as good as Constant Contact, but they will get you started. Once your first campaign brings in revenue, reinvest $12 of it into Constant Contact and make the upgrade. The free trial means you can even test Constant Contact for 60 days before that point anyway.
