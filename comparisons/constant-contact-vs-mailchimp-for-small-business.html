Constant Contact vs Mailchimp: A Direct Feature Comparison

Let's get specific. Here is how the two platforms stack up across every dimension that matters to a small business owner who does not have a dedicated marketing team.

Pricing

Constant Contact's Lite plan starts at $12 per month for up to 500 contacts. The Standard plan, which unlocks automation and A/B testing, starts at $35 per month. Prices scale with your list size but remain predictable.

Mailchimp's free plan allows 500 contacts and 1,000 monthly emails, but it attaches Mailchimp branding to every email and removes most automation. The Essentials plan starts at $13 per month but jumps steeply as your list grows — a 5,000-contact list on Mailchimp's Standard plan costs $75 per month versus $55 per month on Constant Contact's comparable tier.

Ease of Use

Constant Contact wins here without much contest. The interface is built for people who run a plumbing company or a yoga studio, not for marketers who spend their days in dashboards. The onboarding sequence is guided, the template library is industry-organized, and the drag-and-drop editor does not require any technical knowledge to produce a professional-looking email.

Mailchimp has more total features, but that depth creates confusion for new users. The navigation has been restructured multiple times over the past three years, and many small business owners report spending more time figuring out where settings are than actually sending emails.

Customer Support

This is one of the sharpest differences. Constant Contact offers phone support on all paid plans — Monday through Friday, 8am to 10pm ET. You can call a real person, explain your problem, and get help. They also offer live chat and a large self-service knowledge base.

Mailchimp dropped phone support entirely for most plans. Free and Essentials users are limited to email support with response times that can stretch to 24 hours. Standard plan users get live chat during business hours. If something breaks the night before a big campaign send, that matters enormously.

Email Deliverability

Both platforms use dedicated IP pools and follow DKIM and SPF authentication standards. Independent deliverability audits consistently place Constant Contact above 97% inbox placement. Mailchimp sits in the 96–97% range. On a list of 2,000 subscribers, that gap means 20–40 extra emails reaching inboxes instead of spam folders every time you hit send.

Automation

Mailchimp's automation is more advanced at higher plan tiers — it supports multi-step journeys with behavioral triggers, predictive send times, and more complex branching logic. If you are running a sophisticated e-commerce operation with multiple product lines, that depth is valuable.

For most small businesses, Constant Contact's automation is more than enough: welcome series, abandoned cart reminders, birthday emails, and re-engagement sequences all work well and are straightforward to set up without a tutorial.

The Cases Where Mailchimp Is the Better Choice

To be fair to Mailchimp, there are situations where it is the right answer.

If you are a solopreneur with fewer than 500 contacts and no plans to grow significantly, Mailchimp's free plan is genuinely useful — especially if you are comfortable with a slightly more technical interface and do not need phone support. You can send up to 1,000 emails per month for free, which covers a lot of ground for a freelancer or side business just getting started.

If you run a direct-to-consumer e-commerce store with a large SKU catalog, Mailchimp's integration with Shopify and WooCommerce is tighter than Constant Contact's, and its predictive product recommendation feature — which suggests products in email campaigns based on past purchases — is genuinely powerful for conversion optimization.

If you have a dedicated marketing person on staff who can learn the platform and build complex multi-step automations, Mailchimp's Customer Journey Builder at the Standard tier gives you more flexibility than Constant Contact's equivalent.

Outside those specific scenarios, however, the advantages tilt heavily toward Constant Contact for the typical small business owner. You can also read our dedicated Constant Contact vs Mailchimp deep dive if you want to explore edge cases in more detail.

How to Get the Most Out of Constant Contact From Day One

Getting set up correctly in the first week determines whether email marketing works for you or becomes a time sink. Here is exactly what to do.

1. Import your existing contacts immediately

Do not start from zero. If you have a customer list in a spreadsheet, a Gmail contacts export, or a Mailchimp account you are leaving behind, upload it on day one. Constant Contact accepts CSV files directly. Even 50 contacts is enough to run a meaningful first campaign.

2. Send a welcome email within 24 hours of someone subscribing

Set up the automated welcome email before you do anything else. This is the single highest-open-rate email you will ever send — typically 50–80% open rates versus 20–30% for regular campaigns. Use it to tell people what to expect, offer something valuable (a discount code, a helpful tip, a piece of content), and make a strong first impression.

3. Choose a template from your industry, not from scratch

Constant Contact's industry-sorted templates are not just visually appealing — they are structured around what works for that type of business. A restaurant template puts the menu and reservation button front and center. A retail template highlights a featured product. Use the right starting point and you will spend 20 minutes on design instead of two hours.

4. Send on Tuesday or Thursday mornings

Across industries, Tuesday and Thursday sends between 9am and 11am local time consistently outperform other windows for open rates. This is a general rule, not a law — Constant Contact's reporting will show you your specific audience's patterns after a few sends, and you can adjust from there.

5. Track three numbers and ignore the rest at first

New email marketers drown in metrics. Focus on open rate, click rate, and unsubscribe rate. If your open rate is above 20%, your subject lines are working. If your click rate is above 2.5%, your content is compelling. If your unsubscribe rate is above 0.5% per send, your list needs cleaning or your content needs rethinking. Everything else is noise until you have 10+ sends under your belt.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Constant Contact better than Mailchimp for beginners?

Yes. Constant Contact is designed specifically with non-technical users in mind. Its onboarding wizard walks you through list setup, your first template, and your first send in under 30 minutes. Mailchimp has more features but a steeper learning curve that frustrates many first-time senders. If you are new to email marketing, Constant Contact is the faster path to your first campaign.

Does Mailchimp still have a free plan?

Yes, Mailchimp's free plan allows up to 500 contacts and 1,000 emails per month. However, it puts Mailchimp branding on every email, limits automation to one step, and removes phone support entirely. For a business trying to look professional and run effective campaigns, those restrictions add up fast. Constant Contact's 60-day free trial gives you full-feature access without those limitations.

Which tool has better email deliverability?

Both platforms maintain strong deliverability, but Constant Contact consistently scores above 97% inbox placement in independent audits. Mailchimp sits around 96–97%. The difference sounds small, but on a list of 1,000 subscribers, that is 10–20 extra emails landing in inboxes instead of spam folders every single send. Over a year of weekly campaigns, that adds up to thousands of additional people seeing your message.

Can I switch from Mailchimp to Constant Contact without losing my list?

Absolutely. Export your contacts from Mailchimp as a CSV file, then import that file directly into Constant Contact. The whole process takes about 10 minutes. Constant Contact's support team will also walk you through it for free on their phone or chat support line if you get stuck at any point. Your contact history, tags, and segments transfer cleanly in most cases.
